1. Work and the Sign of Work

In everyday speech "work" means effort, but in physics it has a precise meaning: work is energy transferred to or from an object by a force acting through a displacement. Only the component of the force along the direction of motion does work, which is why the angle \(\theta\) between the force and the displacement appears in the definition.

$$W = Fd\cos\theta$$

The factor \(\cos\theta\) controls both the size and the sign of the work, and the sign is the part students most often get wrong. When the force has a component in the same direction as the motion (\(0^\circ \le \theta < 90^\circ\)), \(\cos\theta\) is positive and the force does positive work, adding energy to the object and speeding it up. When the force opposes the motion (\(90^\circ < \theta \le 180^\circ\)), \(\cos\theta\) is negative and the force does negative work, removing energy and slowing the object — friction and a braking force are the classic examples.

The special case \(\theta = 90^\circ\) gives \(\cos 90^\circ = 0\), so a force perpendicular to the motion does zero work. This is why the normal force on a sliding block, the tension on a ball in circular motion, and gravity on a horizontally-moving object all do no work: they change direction, not speed. Work is a scalar measured in joules (\(1\,\text{J} = 1\,\text{N·m}\)), and it can be positive, negative, or zero depending only on this angle.

2. Kinetic Energy and the Work–Energy Theorem

Kinetic energy is the energy an object has because of its motion. It depends on the mass and on the square of the speed, which has a big practical consequence: doubling an object's speed quadruples its kinetic energy (and roughly its stopping distance).

$$KE = \tfrac12 m v^2$$

The work–energy theorem ties work directly to this energy: the net work done on an object by all forces equals its change in kinetic energy.

$$W_{net} = \Delta KE = \tfrac12 m v_f^2 - \tfrac12 m v_i^2$$

This theorem is powerful because it lets you connect force and distance to a change in speed without using the kinematics equations or time at all. The key word is net: you must add up the work done by every force (including negative work from friction and gravity) before setting it equal to \(\Delta KE\). A common pitfall is to use only the work of the applied force; if other forces act, that gives the wrong speed. If the net work is positive the object speeds up, if negative it slows down, and if zero the speed is unchanged — which is exactly the situation for an object moving at constant velocity.

3. Potential Energy — Gravitational and Elastic

Potential energy is stored energy that depends on an object's position or configuration rather than its motion. Gravitational potential energy is the energy stored by lifting an object to a height \(h\) above some chosen reference level; it equals the work you do against gravity to raise it.

$$PE_g = mgh$$

Only changes in height matter physically, so you are free to put the \(h = 0\) reference wherever it makes the algebra simplest — the floor, the ground, or the lowest point of the motion. Elastic potential energy is the energy stored in a stretched or compressed spring, where \(x\) is the displacement from the spring's natural (unstretched) length and \(k\) is the spring constant in N/m.

$$PE_g = mgh$$
$$PE_s = \tfrac12 k x^2$$

Notice the spring energy depends on \(x^2\), so compressing a spring twice as far stores four times the energy — and the sign of \(x\) does not matter, since stretching and compressing by the same amount store the same energy. Both gravity and spring forces are conservative, which means the energy they store can be fully recovered as kinetic energy. This recoverability is exactly what makes the next idea, conservation of mechanical energy, work.

4. Conservation of Mechanical Energy

Mechanical energy is the sum of kinetic and potential energy, \(E_{mech} = KE + PE\). When the only forces doing work are conservative forces (gravity and springs) — that is, when there is no friction, air resistance, or other energy loss — the total mechanical energy stays constant throughout the motion.

$$KE_i + PE_i = KE_f + PE_f$$

Energy simply transforms back and forth between kinetic and potential, like a pendulum trading height for speed and back again, but the total never changes. This gives you an enormously useful shortcut: you can relate the speed and position at one instant directly to the speed and position at any other instant, skipping all the messy details in between. You do not need to know the path, the time, or even the shape of a ramp — only the starting and ending heights and speeds.

The most common setup is an object released from rest at height \(h\): setting the bottom as the reference, \(mgh = \tfrac12 m v^2\), and the mass cancels to give \(v = \sqrt{2gh}\). A key insight is that the mass dropping out means a bowling ball and a marble reach the bottom of a frictionless ramp at the same speed. The catch is the word "frictionless" — the instant a non-conservative force enters, this clean equation no longer balances, and you must account for the lost energy as in the next section.

5. Non-Conservative Forces and Energy "Lost" to Friction

Friction, air resistance, and drag are non-conservative forces: the energy they remove from an object's motion is not stored in a recoverable form but is converted into thermal energy (heat) that spreads into the surfaces and surroundings. Because of this, when friction acts, mechanical energy is not conserved — the object always arrives with less \(KE + PE\) than it started with.

The full bookkeeping is captured by extending the conservation equation to include the work done by non-conservative forces, which is negative because friction opposes the motion:

$$KE_i + PE_i + W_{nc} = KE_f + PE_f$$

A cleaner way to think about it for friction is that the magnitude of the energy turned into heat equals the friction force times the distance traveled, \(|W_{friction}| = f\,d = \mu_k N\,d\). It is important to stress that energy is never truly "lost" or destroyed — the total energy of the universe is still conserved. The mechanical energy has simply transformed into a form (heat) that cannot easily turn back into organized motion.

The practical test on an exam is simple: if the final kinetic energy is less than the initial potential energy (or vice versa), the difference is exactly the energy that went to friction and heat. This is the basis of Example 4 below, where comparing the starting \(PE\) to the final \(KE\) reveals how much energy a rough ramp removed.

6. Power

Power is the rate at which work is done or energy is transferred — it tells you not just how much energy moved, but how fast. Two motors that lift the same load to the same height do the same work, but the one that does it in half the time has twice the power.

$$P = \frac{W}{t}$$
$$P = Fv$$

Power is measured in watts, where \(1\,\text{W} = 1\,\text{J/s}\). The second form, \(P = Fv\), comes from \(P = W/t = Fd/t = F(d/t) = Fv\), and it is extremely handy whenever a force acts on an object moving at a known speed — for instance, the power a car engine delivers while pushing against air resistance at a steady cruising speed.

A useful way to read \(P = Fv\) is that at a fixed engine power, force and speed trade off: this is why a car must downshift (raising the available force) to climb a steep hill, sacrificing top speed for the extra pushing force needed. When power is constant over a time \(t\), the energy delivered is simply \(W = Pt\), which is why your electric bill is measured in kilowatt-hours — a unit of energy, not power.

▶Example 1 — Speed at the bottom of a frictionless ramp

A cart is released from rest at the top of a smooth, frictionless ramp. The top of the ramp is \(2.5\,\text{m}\) higher than the bottom. How fast is the cart moving when it reaches the bottom?

  1. No friction, so mechanical energy is conserved: \(KE_i + PE_i = KE_f + PE_f\).
  2. Choose the bottom as \(h = 0\). The cart starts from rest (\(KE_i = 0\)) and ends at the bottom (\(PE_f = 0\)): \(mgh = \tfrac12 m v^2\).
  3. The mass cancels, leaving \(v = \sqrt{2gh}\). The result does not depend on the cart's mass or the ramp's angle.
  4. Substitute: \(v = \sqrt{2(9.8)(2.5)} = \sqrt{49}\).
  5. Final: \(\boxed{v = 7.0\ \text{m/s}}\).
▶Example 2 — Power to climb stairs

A \(65\,\text{kg}\) student runs up a flight of stairs of total vertical height \(4.0\,\text{m}\) in \(8.0\,\text{s}\) at constant speed. What is the student's average power output against gravity?

  1. At constant speed the kinetic energy does not change, so the work done goes entirely into gravitational potential energy: \(W = mgh\).
  2. Compute the work: \(W = (65)(9.8)(4.0) = 2548\ \text{J}\).
  3. Power is work per unit time: \(P = \dfrac{W}{t} = \dfrac{2548}{8.0}\).
  4. Final: \(\boxed{P = 319\ \text{W}}\) (about \(0.43\) horsepower).
▶Example 3 — A spring launching a block

A spring of constant \(k = 450\ \text{N/m}\) is compressed \(0.18\,\text{m}\) and used to launch a \(1.2\,\text{kg}\) block horizontally across a frictionless surface. What is the block's launch speed as it leaves the spring?

  1. The surface is frictionless and horizontal, so all the elastic potential energy converts to kinetic energy: \(PE_s = KE\).
  2. Write it out: \(\tfrac12 k x^2 = \tfrac12 m v^2\).
  3. Compute the stored energy: \(\tfrac12 (450)(0.18)^2 = \tfrac12(450)(0.0324) = 7.29\ \text{J}\).
  4. Set equal to \(\tfrac12 m v^2\): \(7.29 = \tfrac12(1.2)v^2\), so \(v^2 = \dfrac{2(7.29)}{1.2} = 12.15\).
  5. Final: \(v = \sqrt{12.15} = \boxed{3.49\ \text{m/s}}\).
▶Example 4 — Energy lost to friction on a ramp

A \(2.0\,\text{kg}\) block is released from rest at the top of a rough ramp, \(1.5\,\text{m}\) above the bottom. It reaches the bottom moving at \(4.2\,\text{m/s}\). How much mechanical energy was converted to heat by friction?

  1. Find the energy the block started with — all gravitational PE, since it starts at rest: \(PE_i = mgh = (2.0)(9.8)(1.5) = 29.4\ \text{J}\).
  2. Find the energy it ended with — all kinetic, since it ends at the bottom: \(KE_f = \tfrac12 m v^2 = \tfrac12(2.0)(4.2)^2 = \tfrac12(2.0)(17.64) = 17.64\ \text{J}\).
  3. If the ramp were frictionless the block would have \(29.4\,\text{J}\) of \(KE\) at the bottom; it only has \(17.64\,\text{J}\), and the missing energy went to heat: \(\Delta E = PE_i - KE_f\).
  4. Compute: \(29.4 - 17.64 = 11.76\ \text{J}\).
  5. Final: \(\boxed{11.76\ \text{J}}\) was lost to friction (turned into thermal energy).
▶Example 5 — Work done by a force at an angle

A person pulls a sled \(12\,\text{m}\) across level ground with a rope that makes a \(30^\circ\) angle above the horizontal. The tension in the rope is \(80\,\text{N}\). How much work does the rope do on the sled?

  1. Only the component of force along the displacement does work, so use \(W = Fd\cos\theta\), with \(\theta\) the angle between the force and the horizontal motion.
  2. Identify the values: \(F = 80\ \text{N}\), \(d = 12\ \text{m}\), \(\theta = 30^\circ\), and \(\cos 30^\circ \approx 0.866\).
  3. Substitute: \(W = (80)(12)(0.866) = 960 \times 0.866\).
  4. The vertical component of the tension does zero work because the sled does not move vertically; only the horizontal component counts.
  5. Final: \(W \approx \boxed{831\ \text{J}}\).

1. Momentum: a Vector Quantity

Linear momentum \(\vec p\) measures how much "motion" an object carries, combining how massive it is with how fast it moves. It is defined as the product of mass and velocity:

$$\vec p = m\vec v$$

Because velocity is a vector, momentum is a vector: it points in the same direction as the velocity, and its SI units are \(\mathrm{kg{\cdot}m/s}\). This vector nature is the single most important thing to respect in this unit. In one dimension you must assign a sign convention (say, rightward positive) and stick with it — a \(2\,\mathrm{kg}\) cart moving left at \(3\,\mathrm{m/s}\) has \(p=-6\,\mathrm{kg{\cdot}m/s}\), not \(+6\). In two dimensions you resolve momentum into components and conserve each component independently.

Momentum is closely tied to Newton's second law. Newton actually wrote his law in terms of momentum: the net force equals the time rate of change of momentum, \(\vec F_{net}=\dfrac{d\vec p}{dt}\). When mass is constant this reduces to the familiar \(\vec F=m\vec a\), but the momentum form is more general and is the gateway to impulse.

2. Impulse and the Impulse–Momentum Theorem

Impulse \(\vec J\) is the "push over time" delivered by a force. For a constant force it is the force times the time interval over which it acts, and the impulse–momentum theorem states that this impulse equals the change in momentum:

$$\vec J = \vec F\,\Delta t = \Delta \vec p = m\vec v_f - m\vec v_i$$

When the force varies in time, \(\vec J = \int \vec F\,dt\), which is the area under the force–time graph; the constant-force formula uses the average force. Impulse shares the units of momentum, \(\mathrm{kg{\cdot}m/s}\), which are equivalent to \(\mathrm{N{\cdot}s}\).

The deep practical insight comes from solving for force: \(\vec F_{avg}=\Delta\vec p/\Delta t\). For a given change in momentum, a longer contact time means a smaller average force. This is why an airbag, a crumple zone, or bending your knees on landing protects you: the momentum change is fixed by your speed, but stretching the stopping time over a larger \(\Delta t\) divides that fixed impulse into a gentler force. Conversely, a sudden stop against a rigid dashboard delivers the same \(\Delta p\) in a tiny \(\Delta t\), producing a huge — and injurious — force. A common pitfall is forgetting the vector signs: when a ball bounces back, \(\Delta\vec p\) includes a sign flip, so the magnitude of the change is larger than for a ball that merely stops.

3. Conservation of Momentum

If the net external force on a system is zero, the system's total momentum is constant. This follows directly from the impulse–momentum theorem: with no external impulse, \(\Delta\vec p_{total}=0\). Internal forces — the forces objects in the system exert on each other — always come in Newton's-third-law pairs that are equal and opposite, so they cancel in the total and cannot change the system's overall momentum.

$$\vec p_{i,total}=\vec p_{f,total}\qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad \sum m\vec v_i=\sum m\vec v_f$$

The most important and counterintuitive point: momentum conservation holds even when kinetic energy is lost. In a collision where objects crumple, generate heat, or stick together, mechanical energy is converted to other forms — but momentum has no such escape route, because there is no "thermal momentum." As long as external forces (or their net impulse over the brief collision) are negligible, \(\sum m\vec v\) before equals \(\sum m\vec v\) after. During a quick collision, even gravity and friction can be ignored because they deliver negligible impulse in the short contact time. Always define your system and check that external impulses are small before invoking conservation.

4. Elastic, Inelastic, and Perfectly Inelastic Collisions

Momentum is conserved in every collision (given negligible external impulse); what distinguishes collision types is what happens to kinetic energy.

  • Elastic: total kinetic energy is conserved. Objects bounce off cleanly; think hard steel balls or idealized atomic collisions. Both \(\sum m\vec v\) and \(\sum\tfrac12 mv^2\) are conserved.
  • Inelastic: momentum is conserved but kinetic energy is not — some KE becomes heat, sound, or deformation. Most real collisions are inelastic.
  • Perfectly inelastic: the objects stick together and move with one common velocity afterward. This case loses the maximum possible kinetic energy consistent with conserving momentum.

For a perfectly inelastic collision, the single combined velocity is found directly from momentum conservation:

$$m_1\vec v_1+m_2\vec v_2=(m_1+m_2)\vec v_f$$

5. Center of Mass and Its Motion

The center of mass is the mass-weighted average position of a system — the single point that moves as if all the mass were concentrated there and all external forces acted there. For a set of particles:

$$\vec r_{cm}=\frac{\sum m_i\vec r_i}{\sum m_i},\qquad \vec v_{cm}=\frac{\sum m_i\vec v_i}{\sum m_i}=\frac{\vec p_{total}}{M}$$

The velocity of the center of mass is just the total momentum divided by the total mass, so conservation of momentum is equivalent to saying that the center of mass of an isolated system moves at constant velocity. Internal forces — an exploding firework, two skaters pushing apart, a collision — cannot change the motion of the center of mass; only a net external force can, via \(\vec F_{ext}=M\vec a_{cm}\). This is why the center of mass of a firework shell continues along its original parabolic path even after the shell bursts: gravity is the only external force, so the cm keeps falling as a single projectile while the fragments scatter symmetrically about it.

▶Example 1 — Perfectly inelastic collision: common speed

A \(1500\,\mathrm{kg}\) car moving east at \(20\,\mathrm{m/s}\) rear-ends a \(1000\,\mathrm{kg}\) car at rest. The two cars lock bumpers and move off together. Find their common velocity just after impact.

  1. Take east as positive. Momentum is conserved: \(m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2 = (m_1+m_2)v_f\).
  2. Initial momentum: \((1500)(20)+(1000)(0)=30000\,\mathrm{kg{\cdot}m/s}\).
  3. Combined mass: \(1500+1000=2500\,\mathrm{kg}\).
  4. Solve: \(v_f=\dfrac{30000}{2500}=\boxed{12\ \mathrm{m/s\ east}}\).
▶Example 2 — Average force on a bouncing ball

A \(0.15\,\mathrm{kg}\) ball strikes a wall horizontally at \(12\,\mathrm{m/s}\) and bounces straight back at \(8\,\mathrm{m/s}\). It is in contact with the wall for \(0.020\,\mathrm{s}\). Find the average force the wall exerts on the ball.

  1. Take the rebound direction as positive, so the incoming velocity is \(-12\,\mathrm{m/s}\) and the outgoing is \(+8\,\mathrm{m/s}\).
  2. Change in momentum: \(\Delta p = m(v_f-v_i)=0.15\,[\,8-(-12)\,]=0.15(20)=3.0\,\mathrm{kg{\cdot}m/s}\).
  3. Impulse–momentum theorem: \(F_{avg}=\dfrac{\Delta p}{\Delta t}=\dfrac{3.0}{0.020}=150\,\mathrm{N}\).
  4. The force points in the rebound direction: \(\boxed{F_{avg}=150\ \mathrm{N}}\). Note the sign flip made \(\Delta p\) larger than if the ball had merely stopped.
▶Example 3 — Recoil of a cannon

A \(800\,\mathrm{kg}\) cannon, initially at rest on frictionless ground, fires a \(5\,\mathrm{kg}\) cannonball horizontally at \(120\,\mathrm{m/s}\). Find the recoil velocity of the cannon.

  1. The system (cannon + ball) starts at rest, so total initial momentum is zero. With no external horizontal force, total momentum stays zero.
  2. Conservation: \(0 = m_{ball}v_{ball} + m_{cannon}v_{cannon}\).
  3. Solve: \(v_{cannon}=-\dfrac{m_{ball}v_{ball}}{m_{cannon}}=-\dfrac{(5)(120)}{800}=-0.75\,\mathrm{m/s}\).
  4. The minus sign means the cannon recoils opposite to the ball: \(\boxed{v_{cannon}=0.75\ \mathrm{m/s\ backward}}\).
▶Example 4 — Elastic collision between equal masses

A cart of mass \(m\) moving at \(6\,\mathrm{m/s}\) collides elastically head-on with an identical cart of mass \(m\) at rest. Find the velocity of each cart afterward.

  1. Momentum: \(m(6)+m(0)=mv_1'+mv_2'\Rightarrow v_1'+v_2'=6\).
  2. Kinetic energy (elastic): \(\tfrac12 m(6)^2=\tfrac12 m v_1'^2+\tfrac12 m v_2'^2\Rightarrow v_1'^2+v_2'^2=36\).
  3. Combining these gives \(v_1'v_2'=0\); since the carts cannot pass through each other, the moving cart stops and the struck cart takes off.
  4. Result: \(\boxed{v_1'=0,\ \ v_2'=6\ \mathrm{m/s}}\) — for equal masses in a 1-D elastic collision, the velocities simply exchange.
▶Example 5 — Kinetic energy lost in an inelastic collision

A \(0.50\,\mathrm{kg}\) lump of clay moving at \(8\,\mathrm{m/s}\) strikes and sticks to a \(1.50\,\mathrm{kg}\) block initially at rest on a frictionless surface. How much kinetic energy is lost in the collision?

  1. Find the common final speed from momentum: \(v_f=\dfrac{(0.50)(8)+0}{0.50+1.50}=\dfrac{4.0}{2.0}=2.0\,\mathrm{m/s}\).
  2. Initial KE: \(\tfrac12(0.50)(8)^2=\tfrac12(0.50)(64)=16\,\mathrm{J}\).
  3. Final KE: \(\tfrac12(2.0)(2.0)^2=\tfrac12(2.0)(4.0)=4.0\,\mathrm{J}\).
  4. KE lost: \(16-4.0=\boxed{12\ \mathrm{J}}\) — converted to heat and deformation, while momentum stayed conserved.

1. Angular Kinematics

Rotational motion is described by three quantities that mirror their linear cousins. The angular position \(\theta\) (measured in radians) replaces position \(x\); the angular velocity \(\omega = \tfrac{d\theta}{dt}\) (in \(\mathrm{rad/s}\)) replaces velocity \(v\); and the angular acceleration \(\alpha = \tfrac{d\omega}{dt}\) (in \(\mathrm{rad/s}^2\)) replaces acceleration \(a\). Because every point on a rigid body sweeps through the same angle in the same time, these angular quantities describe the whole object at once, regardless of how big it is.

When \(\alpha\) is constant, the rotational kinematic equations are exact analogs of the constant-acceleration equations you learned in Unit 1. You can derive them by simply swapping \(x\!\to\!\theta\), \(v\!\to\!\omega\), and \(a\!\to\!\alpha\). This analogy is the single most useful shortcut in the unit — anything true for linear motion has a rotational twin.

$$\omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t$$
$$\theta = \theta_0 + \omega_0 t + \tfrac12 \alpha t^2$$
$$\omega^2 = \omega_0^2 + 2\alpha\,\Delta\theta$$
$$\Delta\theta = \tfrac12(\omega_0 + \omega)\,t$$

A common pitfall: these equations only hold when \(\alpha\) is constant, and angles must be in radians, not degrees, for the connection to linear quantities (next section) to work. Counterclockwise is conventionally taken as positive.

2. Connecting Linear and Rotational Motion

A point on a spinning object is also moving through space, so it has an ordinary linear speed. For a point a distance \(r\) from the axis, the arc length it traces is \(s = r\theta\), and differentiating gives the relationship between its linear (tangential) speed and the angular velocity of the body:

$$v = r\omega$$

This is why the rim of a merry-go-round moves faster than a point near the center: same \(\omega\), but larger \(r\). Differentiating again gives the tangential acceleration — the part of the acceleration that speeds the point up or slows it down along its circular path:

$$a_t = r\alpha$$

Do not confuse \(a_t\) with the centripetal acceleration \(a_c = \tfrac{v^2}{r} = r\omega^2\), which points inward toward the axis and changes the direction of motion. Tangential acceleration changes the speed; centripetal acceleration changes the direction. Both exist simultaneously whenever a rotating object is speeding up. Remember: these formulas require \(\theta\), \(\omega\), and \(\alpha\) to be in radian-based units.

3. Torque and the Lever Arm

A force can cause rotation, and the rotational effect of a force is called torque, \(\tau\). Torque depends not just on how hard you push, but on where and in what direction. The magnitude is

$$\tau = rF\sin\theta$$

where \(r\) is the distance from the axis to the point where the force is applied, \(F\) is the force, and \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\vec r\) and \(\vec F\). The quantity \(r\sin\theta\) is the lever arm (or moment arm): the perpendicular distance from the axis to the line of action of the force. This is why a longer wrench loosens a stubborn bolt more easily, and why pushing on a door near its hinges does almost nothing — small lever arm means small torque. A force aimed straight at or away from the axis (\(\theta = 0\) or \(180^\circ\)) produces zero torque; a force applied perpendicular (\(\theta = 90^\circ\)) produces the maximum torque \(\tau = rF\).

Torque is a vector, but in this unit we use a sign convention: torques that tend to rotate the object counterclockwise are positive, clockwise negative. The SI unit is the \(\mathrm{N{\cdot}m}\). Note this is dimensionally the same as a joule, but torque is never called a joule — it is not energy.

4. Rotational Inertia

Mass measures how hard it is to change an object's linear motion. Rotational inertia (or moment of inertia) \(I\) plays the same role for rotation: it measures how hard it is to change an object's spinning. For a collection of point masses, it is defined as

$$I = \sum m_i r_i^2$$

The crucial feature is the \(r^2\): each bit of mass contributes in proportion to the square of its distance from the axis. Mass far from the axis counts much more than mass near it. This is why a figure skater spins faster when she pulls her arms in — she reduces \(r\) for her arms, lowering \(I\), and (by conservation of angular momentum) \(\omega\) rises. It also means the same object has different rotational inertia about different axes. For continuous bodies the sum becomes an integral, giving standard results you should know:

Solid disk/cylinder: $$I = \tfrac12 MR^2$$
Hoop/thin ring: $$I = MR^2$$
Rod about center: $$I = \tfrac{1}{12} ML^2$$
Rod about end: $$I = \tfrac13 ML^2$$
Solid sphere: $$I = \tfrac25 MR^2$$
Point mass: $$I = MR^2$$

Notice the hoop has a larger \(I\) than a disk of the same mass and radius, because all the hoop's mass sits at the maximum distance \(R\), while the disk has mass spread inward. A pitfall: you cannot simply use \(I = MR^2\) for everything — the shape and the chosen axis both matter.

5. Newton's Second Law for Rotation

Just as a net force produces linear acceleration through \(F_{net} = ma\), a net torque produces angular acceleration. The rotational form of Newton's second law is

$$\tau_{net} = I\alpha$$

Every linear quantity has been replaced by its rotational analog: force \(F \to\) torque \(\tau\), mass \(m \to\) rotational inertia \(I\), and linear acceleration \(a \to\) angular acceleration \(\alpha\). To use it, you sum all the torques about a chosen axis (respecting the sign convention) and divide by the rotational inertia about that same axis. The axis you pick must be the same one used to compute both the torques and \(I\).

This law explains why heavy objects with mass concentrated far from the axis are sluggish to spin up: a large \(I\) yields a small \(\alpha\) for a given torque. It also underlies "rolling" problems, where a single object obeys \(F_{net} = ma\) for its center of mass and \(\tau_{net} = I\alpha\) for its rotation simultaneously. A frequent mistake is forgetting that \(\alpha\), not linear \(a\), appears here — the two are linked by \(a_t = r\alpha\) only at the rim.

6. Rotational Equilibrium

An object is in complete static equilibrium when it has no tendency to either translate or rotate. This requires two independent conditions to hold at the same time:

$$\sum F = 0 \qquad \sum \tau = 0$$

The first says the forces balance (no linear acceleration); the second says the torques balance (no angular acceleration). A powerful problem-solving trick: because \(\sum\tau = 0\) about every axis when an object is in equilibrium, you are free to choose the axis wherever it is most convenient. Placing the axis at the point where an unknown force acts makes that force's torque zero (its lever arm is zero), eliminating it from the torque equation and letting you solve directly for another unknown.

  • For an object with weight, gravity acts as if concentrated at the center of mass; use that point when computing gravity's torque.
  • Set clockwise torques equal to counterclockwise torques, or sum signed torques to zero — both work.
  • Equilibrium problems with beams, ladders, and bridges almost always combine one force equation with one cleverly-chosen torque equation.
▶Example 1 — Balancing a see-saw

A child of mass \(40\,\mathrm{kg}\) sits \(1.5\,\mathrm{m}\) to the left of the pivot of a massless see-saw. A second child of mass \(30\,\mathrm{kg}\) sits on the right side. How far from the pivot must the second child sit so the see-saw balances?

  1. Balance means rotational equilibrium: \(\sum\tau = 0\) about the pivot. Each weight is perpendicular to its lever arm, so torque magnitude is \(\tau = mg\,d\).
  2. Set the counterclockwise torque (left child) equal to the clockwise torque (right child): \(m_1 g\, d_1 = m_2 g\, d_2\). The \(g\) cancels.
  3. Solve for \(d_2\): \(d_2 = \dfrac{m_1 d_1}{m_2} = \dfrac{(40)(1.5)}{30}\).
  4. \(d_2 = \dfrac{60}{30} = \boxed{2.0\ \mathrm{m}}\) to the right of the pivot.
▶Example 2 — Torque from an angled force

A force of \(50\,\mathrm{N}\) is applied to the end of a \(0.80\,\mathrm{m}\) wrench, but the force is directed at \(60^\circ\) to the wrench handle rather than perpendicular. Find the torque about the bolt.

  1. Use \(\tau = rF\sin\theta\), where \(\theta\) is the angle between the handle (the \(\vec r\) direction) and the force.
  2. Identify values: \(r = 0.80\,\mathrm{m}\), \(F = 50\,\mathrm{N}\), \(\theta = 60^\circ\), so \(\sin 60^\circ = 0.866\).
  3. Compute: \(\tau = (0.80)(50)(0.866) = 34.6\,\mathrm{N{\cdot}m}\).
  4. Rounding: \(\tau \approx \boxed{35\ \mathrm{N{\cdot}m}}\). (Had the force been perpendicular, the torque would have been the full \(40\,\mathrm{N{\cdot}m}\).)
▶Example 3 — Spinning up a disk

A solid disk of mass \(M = 4.0\,\mathrm{kg}\) and radius \(R = 0.25\,\mathrm{m}\) is free to rotate about its central axis. A rope wrapped around its rim is pulled with a constant tangential force of \(12\,\mathrm{N}\). Find the angular acceleration of the disk. (Solid disk: \(I = \tfrac12 MR^2\).)

  1. The rope is tangent to the rim, so its lever arm is the full radius \(R\): the torque is \(\tau = FR = (12)(0.25) = 3.0\,\mathrm{N{\cdot}m}\).
  2. Compute the rotational inertia: \(I = \tfrac12 MR^2 = \tfrac12 (4.0)(0.25)^2 = \tfrac12(4.0)(0.0625) = 0.125\,\mathrm{kg{\cdot}m}^2\).
  3. Apply Newton's second law for rotation: \(\alpha = \dfrac{\tau_{net}}{I} = \dfrac{3.0}{0.125}\).
  4. \(\alpha = \boxed{24\ \mathrm{rad/s}^2}\).
▶Example 4 — Angular kinematics of a wheel

A wheel starts from rest and accelerates uniformly. After turning through \(20\,\mathrm{rad}\) it reaches an angular velocity of \(8.0\,\mathrm{rad/s}\). Find its angular acceleration, then the time taken.

  1. Choose the kinematic equation that omits time: \(\omega^2 = \omega_0^2 + 2\alpha\,\Delta\theta\). Here \(\omega_0 = 0\), \(\omega = 8.0\,\mathrm{rad/s}\), \(\Delta\theta = 20\,\mathrm{rad}\).
  2. Solve for \(\alpha\): \(\alpha = \dfrac{\omega^2 - \omega_0^2}{2\,\Delta\theta} = \dfrac{(8.0)^2}{2(20)} = \dfrac{64}{40} = \boxed{1.6\ \mathrm{rad/s}^2}\).
  3. For the time, use \(\omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t\): \(t = \dfrac{\omega - \omega_0}{\alpha} = \dfrac{8.0}{1.6}\).
  4. \(t = \boxed{5.0\ \mathrm{s}}\).
▶Example 5 — Uniform beam on two supports

A uniform beam of mass \(20\,\mathrm{kg}\) and length \(4.0\,\mathrm{m}\) rests horizontally on two supports, one at the far left end (A) and one at the far right end (B). A \(60\,\mathrm{kg}\) person stands \(1.0\,\mathrm{m}\) from the left end. Find the upward force exerted by support B.

  1. Use \(\sum\tau = 0\). Take the axis at support A so that \(F_A\) has zero lever arm and drops out, leaving only \(F_B\) as the unknown force.
  2. The beam's weight \((20)(9.8) = 196\,\mathrm{N}\) acts at its center, \(2.0\,\mathrm{m}\) from A. The person's weight \((60)(9.8) = 588\,\mathrm{N}\) acts \(1.0\,\mathrm{m}\) from A. \(F_B\) acts at \(4.0\,\mathrm{m}\) from A.
  3. Torque balance about A (counterclockwise from \(F_B\) equals clockwise from the weights): \(F_B(4.0) = (196)(2.0) + (588)(1.0)\).
  4. \(F_B(4.0) = 392 + 588 = 980\,\mathrm{N{\cdot}m}\), so \(F_B = \dfrac{980}{4.0}\).
  5. \(F_B = \boxed{245\ \mathrm{N}}\) upward.

1. Angular Momentum L = Iω

Just as linear momentum \(p = mv\) measures "how much motion" a moving mass carries, angular momentum measures how much rotational motion a spinning object carries. For a rigid body rotating about a fixed axis it is the product of the rotational inertia \(I\) and the angular velocity \(\omega\):

$$L = I\omega$$

The rotational inertia \(I\) plays the role that mass plays in linear motion: it tells you how stubbornly an object resists changes in its spin. But unlike mass, \(I\) is not fixed by the amount of material alone — it depends on how that material is distributed relative to the axis. Moving mass farther out increases \(I\) sharply because \(I\) depends on the square of the distance. A single point mass has \(I = mr^2\); extended shapes are sums of such terms, giving the disk \(\tfrac12 MR^2\), the hoop \(MR^2\), and the solid sphere \(\tfrac25 MR^2\).

Angular momentum is a vector pointing along the rotation axis, and its units are \(\mathrm{kg{\cdot}m}^2\mathrm{/s}\). The deep reason \(L\) matters is the rotational analog of Newton's second law: a net external torque is what changes angular momentum, \(\tau_{net} = \dfrac{\Delta L}{\Delta t}\). When no external torque acts, \(L\) cannot change — the foundation of the next section.

$$\tau_{net} = \frac{\Delta L}{\Delta t}$$

2. Conservation of Angular Momentum

If the net external torque on a system is zero, its total angular momentum stays constant. This is conservation of angular momentum, and it is one of the most powerful tools in rotational physics because it lets you connect a "before" state to an "after" state without knowing any of the messy details in between:

$$L_i = L_f \qquad I_i\omega_i = I_f\omega_f$$

The classic demonstration is the figure skater. A skater spinning with arms outstretched has a large rotational inertia because much of her mass (arms, hands) sits far from the spin axis. When she pulls her arms in, that mass moves close to the axis, so \(I\) drops dramatically. Her internal muscular forces are internal to the system and the small friction from the ice provides negligible torque, so \(L\) is conserved. With \(L = I\omega\) fixed, a smaller \(I\) forces a larger \(\omega\) — she spins faster, sometimes several times faster, simply by rearranging her own mass.

The key conceptual point: she does not push against anything outside herself, yet she speeds up. Nothing external sped her rotation; the product \(I\omega\) was simply redistributed. The same principle explains why a collapsing star spins up into a pulsar, why a diver tucks to flip faster, and why a planet's orbital sweep stays constant (Kepler's second law).

3. Rotational Kinetic Energy ½Iω²

A spinning object stores kinetic energy in its rotation. Summing \(\tfrac12 mv^2\) over every particle (each with \(v = \omega r\)) collapses to a clean expression mirroring \(\tfrac12 mv^2\):

$$KE_{rot} = \tfrac12 I\omega^2$$

Here is a subtlety that confuses many students. When the skater of Section 2 pulls her arms in, angular momentum \(L = I\omega\) is conserved — but her kinetic energy is not. We can see this by writing the energy in terms of the conserved quantity: since \(L = I\omega\), we have \(\omega = L/I\), so

$$KE_{rot} = \tfrac12 I\omega^2 = \frac{L^2}{2I}.$$

With \(L\) fixed, decreasing \(I\) makes \(KE_{rot}\) increase. The skater spins faster and ends up with more rotational kinetic energy. Where did the extra energy come from? From the skater herself: her muscles do positive work pulling her arms inward against the outward "centrifugal" tendency of her arms, which want to fly outward. That biological work is converted into rotational kinetic energy.

So conservation of angular momentum and conservation of energy are separate bookkeeping rules. \(L\) is conserved here because there is no external torque; mechanical energy is not conserved because an internal source (muscles) added energy. Letting the arms back out reverses it — the arms do negative work and the rotational energy drops back down.

4. Rolling Without Slipping

An object that rolls without slipping moves and spins at the same time, with the two motions locked together. The contact point does not skid, which ties the translational speed of the center to the angular speed:

$$v = r\omega$$

Because it both translates and rotates, a rolling body stores kinetic energy in both forms. Its total kinetic energy is the sum of the translational energy of its center of mass and the rotational energy about that center:

$$KE_{total} = \tfrac12 mv^2 + \tfrac12 I\omega^2$$

This split explains the famous ramp race. Release a hoop and a disk of any masses and radii from the same height; the disk wins every time. As each shape rolls down, gravity converts the same potential energy \(mgh\) into kinetic energy — but that energy must be shared between translation and rotation. Substituting \(\omega = v/r\), the rotational share is \(\tfrac12 I (v/r)^2\). A hoop (\(I = MR^2\)) sends a large fraction of the energy into spin, leaving less for translation, so its center moves slowly. A disk (\(I = \tfrac12 MR^2\)) keeps more for translation and arrives first. The solid sphere (\(I = \tfrac25 MR^2\)) is even more translation-heavy and beats them both.

Notice the winner does not depend on mass or radius at all — only on the shape factor in \(I = cMR^2\). The smaller \(c\), the more the mass hugs the axis, the less energy goes into spin, and the faster the object reaches the bottom.

5. Orbiting Satellites

A satellite in a circular orbit is in constant free fall: gravity continuously pulls it toward the planet, bending its straight-line path into a circle. Gravity is the centripetal force here, so we set the gravitational pull equal to the mass times the centripetal acceleration \(v^2/r\):

$$\frac{GMm}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

The satellite's own mass \(m\) cancels from both sides — a heavy satellite and a light one orbit at the same radius with the same speed. Solving for \(v\) gives the orbital speed:

$$v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$

5b. Why Distant Orbits Are Slower

The orbital-speed result \(v = \sqrt{GM/r}\) carries a counterintuitive lesson: the farther out a satellite orbits, the slower it moves. Because \(r\) sits in the denominator under the square root, doubling the orbital radius cuts the speed by a factor of \(\sqrt{2}\). A low-Earth-orbit satellite races around at nearly \(8\ \mathrm{km/s}\), while the far-out Moon ambles along at about \(1\ \mathrm{km/s}\).

Physically this makes sense: gravity weakens with distance (\(\propto 1/r^2\)), so far from the planet there is only a feeble inward pull available to bend the path. A weak centripetal force can only sustain a gentle, slow curve — a fast satellite out there would simply fly off. So weaker gravity at large \(r\) demands a smaller orbital speed, and the period grows even faster, exactly as Kepler's third law describes.

▶Example 1 — Skater pulls in her arms

A figure skater spins with arms outstretched at \(\omega_i = 1.8\ \mathrm{rad/s}\), with rotational inertia \(I_i = 4.5\ \mathrm{kg{\cdot}m}^2\). She pulls her arms in, reducing her rotational inertia to \(I_f = 1.2\ \mathrm{kg{\cdot}m}^2\). Find her new angular speed.

  1. No external torque acts (ice friction is negligible, muscles are internal), so angular momentum is conserved: \(I_i\omega_i = I_f\omega_f\).
  2. Solve for the new angular speed: \(\omega_f = \dfrac{I_i\omega_i}{I_f}\).
  3. Substitute: \(\omega_f = \dfrac{(4.5)(1.8)}{1.2} = \dfrac{8.1}{1.2}\).
  4. Final: \(\boxed{\omega_f = 6.75\ \mathrm{rad/s}}\) — she spins about 3.75 times faster.
▶Example 2 — Rotational KE of a disk

A solid disk of mass \(M = 2.0\ \mathrm{kg}\) and radius \(R = 0.30\ \mathrm{m}\) spins about its central axis at \(\omega = 12\ \mathrm{rad/s}\). A solid disk has \(I = \tfrac12 MR^2\). Find its rotational kinetic energy.

  1. Rotational inertia: \(I = \tfrac12 MR^2 = \tfrac12 (2.0)(0.30)^2 = \tfrac12 (2.0)(0.09) = 0.090\ \mathrm{kg{\cdot}m}^2\).
  2. Rotational kinetic energy: \(KE_{rot} = \tfrac12 I\omega^2\).
  3. Substitute: \(KE_{rot} = \tfrac12 (0.090)(12)^2 = \tfrac12 (0.090)(144)\).
  4. Final: \(\boxed{KE_{rot} = 6.48\ \mathrm{J}}\).
▶Example 3 — Child lands on a merry-go-round

A playground merry-go-round is a solid disk of mass \(M = 120\ \mathrm{kg}\) and radius \(R = 2.0\ \mathrm{m}\) rotating freely at \(\omega_i = 0.80\ \mathrm{rad/s}\). A child of mass \(m = 40\ \mathrm{kg}\) drops straight down onto its edge and holds on. Treating the child as a point mass (\(I = mr^2\)), find the new angular speed. The disk has \(I = \tfrac12 MR^2\).

  1. The child lands vertically, exerting no torque about the axis, so angular momentum is conserved: \(I_{disk}\,\omega_i = (I_{disk} + I_{child})\,\omega_f\).
  2. Disk: \(I_{disk} = \tfrac12 MR^2 = \tfrac12 (120)(2.0)^2 = 240\ \mathrm{kg{\cdot}m}^2\).
  3. Child at the edge: \(I_{child} = mr^2 = (40)(2.0)^2 = 160\ \mathrm{kg{\cdot}m}^2\).
  4. Solve: \(\omega_f = \dfrac{I_{disk}\,\omega_i}{I_{disk}+I_{child}} = \dfrac{(240)(0.80)}{240+160} = \dfrac{192}{400}\).
  5. Final: \(\boxed{\omega_f = 0.48\ \mathrm{rad/s}}\).
▶Example 4 — Rotational fraction of a rolling sphere

A solid sphere rolls without slipping down a ramp. What fraction of its total kinetic energy is rotational? A solid sphere has \(I = \tfrac25 MR^2\), and rolling without slipping means \(v = R\omega\).

  1. Rotational part: \(KE_{rot} = \tfrac12 I\omega^2 = \tfrac12\left(\tfrac25 MR^2\right)\left(\dfrac{v}{R}\right)^2 = \tfrac15 Mv^2\).
  2. Translational part: \(KE_{trans} = \tfrac12 Mv^2\).
  3. Total: \(KE_{total} = \tfrac15 Mv^2 + \tfrac12 Mv^2 = \tfrac{2}{10}Mv^2 + \tfrac{5}{10}Mv^2 = \tfrac{7}{10}Mv^2\).
  4. Fraction rotational: \(\dfrac{KE_{rot}}{KE_{total}} = \dfrac{\tfrac15 Mv^2}{\tfrac{7}{10}Mv^2} = \dfrac{2/10}{7/10} = \dfrac{2}{7}\).
  5. Final: \(\boxed{\dfrac{2}{7} \approx 0.286}\) — about \(28.6\%\) of the energy is in rotation, independent of mass and radius.
▶Example 5 — Orbital speed at a given radius

A satellite orbits Earth in a circular orbit of radius \(r = 7.0\times10^{6}\ \mathrm{m}\) (measured from Earth's center). For Earth, \(GM = 3.986\times10^{14}\ \mathrm{m^3/s^2}\). Find the satellite's orbital speed.

  1. Gravity supplies the centripetal force: \(\dfrac{GMm}{r^2} = \dfrac{mv^2}{r}\). The satellite mass \(m\) cancels.
  2. Solve for speed: \(v = \sqrt{\dfrac{GM}{r}}\).
  3. Substitute: \(v = \sqrt{\dfrac{3.986\times10^{14}}{7.0\times10^{6}}} = \sqrt{5.69\times10^{7}}\).
  4. Final: \(\boxed{v \approx 7.5\times10^{3}\ \mathrm{m/s}}\) (about \(7546\ \mathrm{m/s}\)).

1. What makes motion simple harmonic

An oscillation is simple harmonic motion (SHM) when the net force always points back toward a single equilibrium position and grows in direct proportion to how far the object has been displaced from that position. We write this restoring condition as \(F = -kx\), where \(x\) is the displacement from equilibrium and \(k\) is a positive constant. The crucial features are both packed into that one equation: the magnitude of the force is proportional to \(x\) (so pulling twice as far gives twice the restoring force), and the minus sign means the force is always opposite to the displacement, perpetually trying to drive the object back to \(x = 0\).

This linear restoring force is what guarantees the motion is sinusoidal in time. Because force is proportional to displacement, Newton's second law gives an acceleration \(a = -(k/m)x\), and the only functions whose second derivative is a negative constant times themselves are sines and cosines. That is why every true SHM oscillator traces out \(x(t) = A\cos(\omega t + \phi)\), repeating with a fixed period no matter where in the swing you start measuring.

A mass on a spring is the textbook example, where \(k\) is literally the spring constant. A pendulum is SHM only approximately: its restoring force is \(F = -mg\sin\theta\), which is not proportional to \(\theta\). But for small angles \(\sin\theta \approx \theta\), the force becomes linear in the displacement and the motion becomes simple harmonic. That small-angle approximation is the hidden assumption behind every pendulum period formula.

2. Period and frequency formulas

The period \(T\) is the time for one complete cycle; the frequency \(f = 1/T\) counts cycles per second (in hertz). The angular frequency is \(\omega = 2\pi f = 2\pi/T\). For the two oscillators you must know, the periods are:

$$T_{spring} = 2\pi\sqrt{\tfrac{m}{k}}$$
$$T_{pend} = 2\pi\sqrt{\tfrac{L}{g}}$$

Read carefully what each formula contains, because the AP exam loves testing what the period does not depend on:

  • The mass–spring period depends only on the mass \(m\) and the stiffness \(k\). A heavier mass is harder to accelerate, so it oscillates more slowly; a stiffer spring pulls back harder, so it oscillates faster. It does not depend on the amplitude or on gravity.
  • The pendulum period depends only on the length \(L\) and the local gravitational field \(g\). Strikingly, it does not depend on the mass of the bob, nor (for small swings) on the amplitude.

Why is the pendulum independent of mass? Gravity supplies the restoring force \(mg\sin\theta\), but the same mass \(m\) also appears in the inertia \(ma\). The mass cancels, exactly as it does in free fall, leaving a period set purely by \(L\) and \(g\).

3. Energy in simple harmonic motion

In an ideal oscillator with no friction, the total mechanical energy is conserved and simply trades back and forth between kinetic and potential forms. For a mass on a spring the spring's potential energy is \(U = \tfrac{1}{2}kx^2\) and the kinetic energy is \(KE = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2\). Their sum is constant throughout the motion:

$$E = \tfrac{1}{2}kx^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}kA^2$$

The right-hand expression evaluates the total energy at the amplitude \(x = A\), where the mass is momentarily at rest so all the energy is potential. This single fact, that \(E = \tfrac{1}{2}kA^2\), tells you the energy of an oscillator scales with the square of its amplitude: double the amplitude and you quadruple the energy.

Tracking where the energy lives explains the motion's character. At the turning points (\(x = \pm A\)) the displacement is largest, so \(U\) is maximum, \(v = 0\), and the object is instantaneously still before reversing. At the equilibrium point (\(x = 0\)) the potential energy is zero, so all the energy is kinetic and the object is moving its fastest. Everywhere in between, the energy is shared, KE rising as the mass speeds up toward the center and falling as it climbs back out toward a turning point.

4. Position, velocity, and acceleration through a cycle

If we start the oscillator from rest at the amplitude, its motion is described by three linked sinusoids that are out of step with one another:

$$x(t) = A\cos(\omega t)$$
$$v(t) = -A\omega\sin(\omega t)$$

The acceleration follows directly from the restoring force, \(a = F/m = -(k/m)x\). Because \(k/m = \omega^2\), this is exactly \(a = -\omega^2 x\): the acceleration is always proportional to the displacement and points the opposite way. That relationship is the mathematical signature of SHM, and it tells you where each quantity peaks:

  • At the turning points (\(x = \pm A\)): displacement is maximum, velocity is zero, and acceleration has its largest magnitude \(a_{max} = \omega^2 A\), since the restoring force is strongest there.
  • At equilibrium (\(x = 0\)): displacement is zero, so acceleration is zero (no net force), yet velocity reaches its maximum magnitude \(v_{max} = \omega A\).

Notice that velocity and acceleration are never large at the same instant. The mass moves fastest exactly where it is not accelerating, and it accelerates hardest exactly where it has momentarily stopped. This 90-degree phase offset is why the position and velocity graphs are a cosine and a sine of the same cycle.

5. What changes the period

A recurring AP theme is distinguishing what genuinely alters the period from what merely changes the size of the swing. The single most surprising result is amplitude independence: for both the mass–spring system and the small-angle pendulum, increasing the amplitude does not change the period. A larger amplitude means the object travels farther in each cycle, but it also moves faster (the larger restoring force gives larger speeds), and these two effects cancel exactly. This isochronism is why a pendulum clock keeps steady time even as its swing slowly dies down, and it holds only while the swing stays small enough that \(\sin\theta \approx \theta\).

For the mass–spring system, what does change the period is the mass and the spring constant, through \(T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}\). Because the period depends on \(\sqrt{m}\), the relationship is nonlinear: to double the period you must quadruple the mass. The maximum speed, found by setting all the energy kinetic at equilibrium, makes the amplitude's role explicit:

$$\tfrac{1}{2}kA^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}mv_{max}^2 \;\Rightarrow\; v_{max} = A\sqrt{\tfrac{k}{m}}$$

So amplitude does not touch the period, but it scales the maximum speed in direct proportion. For the pendulum, the period is governed by \(L\) and \(g\): lengthening the string slows it down (longer period), while a stronger gravitational field speeds it up (shorter period). Taking the same pendulum to the Moon, where \(g\) is about one-sixth of Earth's, lengthens its period by a factor of \(\sqrt{6}\), because \(T \propto 1/\sqrt{g}\).

▶Example 1 — Period of a simple pendulum

A simple pendulum has a string of length \(L = 0.80\ \text{m}\) and swings through a small angle. Find its period on Earth, where \(g = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2\).

  1. Use the pendulum period formula: \(T = 2\pi\sqrt{\tfrac{L}{g}}\). The bob's mass and the (small) amplitude are irrelevant.
  2. Substitute: \(T = 2\pi\sqrt{\dfrac{0.80}{9.8}} = 2\pi\sqrt{0.08163}\).
  3. Evaluate the root: \(\sqrt{0.08163} = 0.2857\ \text{s}\).
  4. Multiply: \(T = 2\pi(0.2857) = \boxed{1.80\ \text{s}}\).
▶Example 2 — Period of a mass–spring system

A block of mass \(m = 0.50\ \text{kg}\) is attached to a spring with spring constant \(k = 200\ \text{N/m}\) on a frictionless surface. Find the period of its oscillation.

  1. Use the mass–spring period formula: \(T = 2\pi\sqrt{\tfrac{m}{k}}\). Gravity and amplitude do not enter.
  2. Substitute: \(T = 2\pi\sqrt{\dfrac{0.50}{200}} = 2\pi\sqrt{0.0025}\).
  3. Evaluate the root: \(\sqrt{0.0025} = 0.050\ \text{s}\).
  4. Multiply: \(T = 2\pi(0.050) = \boxed{0.31\ \text{s}}\).
▶Example 3 — Maximum speed from energy

The same block (\(m = 0.50\ \text{kg}\), \(k = 200\ \text{N/m}\)) is pulled to an amplitude of \(A = 0.10\ \text{m}\) and released. Find its maximum speed using energy conservation.

  1. At the amplitude all the energy is potential; at equilibrium all of it is kinetic. Set them equal: \(\tfrac{1}{2}kA^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}mv_{max}^2\).
  2. Solve for the speed: \(v_{max} = A\sqrt{\dfrac{k}{m}}\).
  3. Compute the ratio: \(\dfrac{k}{m} = \dfrac{200}{0.50} = 400\ \text{s}^{-2}\), so \(\sqrt{400} = 20\ \text{s}^{-1}\).
  4. Multiply by the amplitude: \(v_{max} = 0.10 \times 20 = \boxed{2.0\ \text{m/s}}\).
▶Example 4 — A pendulum on another planet

A pendulum has a period of \(T_E = 2.0\ \text{s}\) on Earth (\(g_E = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2\)). It is taken to a planet where the gravitational field is \(g_P = 4.9\ \text{m/s}^2\), exactly half of Earth's. Find its new period, without computing the length.

  1. The length \(L\) is unchanged, and \(T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}\), so \(T \propto 1/\sqrt{g}\). Take a ratio: \(\dfrac{T_P}{T_E} = \sqrt{\dfrac{g_E}{g_P}}\).
  2. Insert the field ratio: \(\dfrac{g_E}{g_P} = \dfrac{9.8}{4.9} = 2\), so \(\dfrac{T_P}{T_E} = \sqrt{2} = 1.414\).
  3. Multiply by the Earth period: \(T_P = 1.414 \times 2.0\ \text{s}\).
  4. The weaker gravity gives a longer period: \(T_P = \boxed{2.8\ \text{s}}\).
▶Example 5 — Quadrupling the mass on a spring

A mass on a spring oscillates with period \(T_0 = 0.60\ \text{s}\). The mass is replaced by one four times as large (\(m \rightarrow 4m\)) on the same spring. Find the new period.

  1. Since \(T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}\) with \(k\) fixed, the period scales as \(T \propto \sqrt{m}\). Take a ratio: \(\dfrac{T_{new}}{T_0} = \sqrt{\dfrac{4m}{m}} = \sqrt{4} = 2\).
  2. Quadrupling the mass therefore doubles the period, not quadruples it, because of the square root.
  3. Multiply: \(T_{new} = 2 \times 0.60 = \boxed{1.2\ \text{s}}\).

1. Density and Pressure

Density measures how much mass is packed into a given volume, \(\rho = \tfrac{m}{V}\). It is an intrinsic property of a material: a steel ball and a steel beam have wildly different masses, but the same density. Water has \(\rho_{water}=1000\ \mathrm{kg/m^3}\), meaning one cubic meter of water has a mass of 1000 kg.

Pressure is the force exerted perpendicular to a surface per unit area, \(P = \tfrac{F}{A}\). Its SI unit is the pascal, \(1\ \text{Pa} = 1\ \mathrm{N/m^2}\). Pressure is a scalar — at any point in a fluid it pushes equally in all directions, so it has no single direction of its own.

The same force spread over a smaller area produces a larger pressure. This is why a sharp knife (tiny contact area) cuts easily while the same force from a blunt edge does not, and why snowshoes (large area) keep you from sinking. In a fluid, pressure is the key quantity because a fluid cannot sustain a shear force — it simply flows — so it transmits force through pressure.

$$\rho = \tfrac{m}{V}$$
$$P = \tfrac{F}{A}$$

2. Pressure and Depth

Dive into water and your ears feel the increasing pressure. The fluid pressure at a depth \(h\) below the surface is given by \(P = P_0 + \rho g h\), where \(P_0\) is the pressure pushing down on the surface (usually atmospheric, \(P_0=1.0\times10^5\ \text{Pa}\)). The term \(\rho g h\) is the extra pressure from the weight of the fluid column above you.

Why only depth? Picture an imaginary column of fluid of cross-sectional area \(A\) reaching from the surface down to depth \(h\). Its weight is \(W = mg = \rho (A h) g\). That weight is supported by the pressure pushing up on the bottom of the column, so the added pressure is \(\tfrac{W}{A} = \rho g h\) — the area \(A\) cancels out. The pressure depends only on the vertical depth, the fluid density, and \(g\). It does not depend on the shape of the container or the total amount of fluid: a narrow tube and a wide lake have the same pressure at the same depth. This is the "hydrostatic paradox."

Gauge vs. absolute pressure. Absolute pressure is the total pressure, \(P = P_0 + \rho g h\). Gauge pressure is the amount above atmospheric, \(P_{gauge} = \rho g h\) — it is what a tire gauge or blood-pressure cuff reads, since those measure the difference from the surrounding air.

$$P = P_0 + \rho g h \qquad P_{gauge} = P - P_0 = \rho g h$$

3. Pascal's Principle and Hydraulics

Pascal's principle states that a pressure change applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted undiminished to every point in the fluid and to the walls of its container. Because the fluid carries the same pressure everywhere, we can use it to multiply force.

In a hydraulic lift, a small input piston of area \(A_1\) and a large output piston of area \(A_2\) are connected by fluid. Pushing on the small piston with force \(F_1\) creates a pressure \(P = \tfrac{F_1}{A_1}\). That same pressure acts on the large piston, producing \(F_2 = P A_2 = F_1 \tfrac{A_2}{A_1}\). Make the output area 50 times larger and you get 50 times the force — this is how a car is lifted with the push of a hand.

Work is still conserved. You get force for free, but not work. The incompressible fluid pushed out from under the small piston must reappear under the large one: \(A_1 d_1 = A_2 d_2\), so the small piston moves a long distance \(d_1\) while the large piston moves a tiny distance \(d_2\). The input work \(F_1 d_1\) equals the output work \(F_2 d_2\) — energy is not created, only traded. You trade distance for force, exactly like a lever.

$$\frac{F_1}{A_1} = \frac{F_2}{A_2} \qquad F_2 = F_1\frac{A_2}{A_1}$$

4. Buoyancy and Archimedes' Principle

Archimedes' principle states that the upward buoyant force on an object in a fluid equals the weight of the fluid the object displaces: \(F_b = \rho_{fluid}\, g\, V_{displaced}\).

Why does this happen? Pressure increases with depth, so the fluid pushes harder on the bottom of a submerged object than on its top. That difference in pressure forces, integrated over the surface, gives a net upward push. Replace the object with an equal volume of the surrounding fluid: that fluid blob just floats in place, neither rising nor sinking, so the upward buoyant force on it must exactly balance its own weight, \(\rho_{fluid} g V\). The buoyant force depends only on the volume of fluid pushed aside, not on what the object is made of.

An object sinks if its density exceeds the fluid's, floats if it is less. A fully submerged object displaces its entire volume; a floating object displaces only enough to support its weight.

  • Submerged: \(V_{displaced} = V_{object}\).
  • Floating: \(F_b = mg\), so only part of the object is under the surface.
  • Apparent weight in fluid: \(W_{apparent} = mg - F_b\).

$$F_b = \rho_{fluid}\, g\, V_{displaced}$$

5. Continuity and Conservation of Mass

For a fluid flowing steadily through a pipe, mass cannot pile up or vanish — whatever flows in must flow out. For an incompressible fluid (constant density), this means the volume flow rate is the same everywhere: \(A_1 v_1 = A_2 v_2\). The product \(Av\) is the volume passing a point per second.

Why does a narrowing pipe speed the flow up? If the same volume of water per second must squeeze through a smaller opening, it has no choice but to move faster. Halve the cross-sectional area and the speed doubles. This is why you partially cover a hose nozzle to make the water shoot out faster, and why a river runs quickest where it narrows. The speed and area are inversely proportional: \(v \propto \tfrac{1}{A}\).

$$A_1 v_1 = A_2 v_2$$

6. Bernoulli's Equation

Bernoulli's equation is conservation of energy for a flowing fluid, written per unit volume. Along a streamline of an ideal fluid:

$$P + \tfrac12 \rho v^2 + \rho g y = \text{constant}$$

The three terms are the pressure energy, the kinetic energy per unit volume \(\tfrac12 \rho v^2\), and the gravitational potential energy per unit volume \(\rho g y\). If you raise one term, another must drop so the sum stays fixed.

The pressure–speed trade-off. At the same height, where a fluid moves faster, its pressure is lower; where it moves slower, its pressure is higher. This follows directly from the equation: if \(\tfrac12 \rho v^2\) goes up, \(P\) must come down. The physical reason is energy — to speed a fluid parcel up as it enters a fast region, a net force must do work on it, and that force comes from a drop in pressure (high pressure behind, low pressure ahead).

Where you see it. Airplane lift: air travels faster over the curved top of a wing, lowering the pressure there, so the higher pressure below pushes the wing up. A spinning curveball drags air around with it, making the air faster on one side; the lower pressure on that side curves the ball's path. The same effect makes shower curtains billow inward and roofs lift off in high winds.

▶Example 1 — Pressure at depth in a lake

A scuba diver is \(15\ \text{m}\) below the surface of a freshwater lake. Find (a) the gauge pressure and (b) the absolute pressure at that depth. Use \(\rho_{water}=1000\ \mathrm{kg/m^3}\), \(g=9.8\ \mathrm{m/s^2}\), \(P_0=1.0\times10^5\ \text{Pa}\).

  1. Gauge pressure is the pressure from the water column alone: \(P_{gauge} = \rho g h\).
  2. \(P_{gauge} = (1000)(9.8)(15) = 147000\ \text{Pa}\), so \(\boxed{P_{gauge} \approx 1.47\times10^5\ \text{Pa}}\).
  3. Absolute pressure adds atmospheric: \(P = P_0 + \rho g h = 1.0\times10^5 + 1.47\times10^5\).
  4. \(\boxed{P \approx 2.47\times10^5\ \text{Pa}}\).
▶Example 2 — Hydraulic lift force

In a hydraulic lift, the input piston has a radius of \(2.0\ \text{cm}\) and the output piston has a radius of \(20\ \text{cm}\). If you push on the input piston with a force of \(150\ \text{N}\), what force is exerted on the output piston?

  1. The force scales with the ratio of areas, and area \(\propto r^2\): \(\dfrac{A_2}{A_1} = \left(\dfrac{r_2}{r_1}\right)^2\).
  2. \(\dfrac{A_2}{A_1} = \left(\dfrac{20}{2.0}\right)^2 = 10^2 = 100\).
  3. \(F_2 = F_1\dfrac{A_2}{A_1} = (150)(100)\).
  4. \(\boxed{F_2 = 1.5\times10^4\ \text{N}}\).
▶Example 3 — Speed in a narrowed pipe

Water flows at \(2.0\ \mathrm{m/s}\) through a pipe of cross-sectional area \(0.080\ \mathrm{m^2}\). The pipe narrows to an area of \(0.020\ \mathrm{m^2}\). What is the water's speed in the narrow section?

  1. Continuity for an incompressible fluid: \(A_1 v_1 = A_2 v_2\).
  2. Solve for \(v_2\): \(v_2 = v_1\dfrac{A_1}{A_2} = (2.0)\dfrac{0.080}{0.020}\).
  3. \(v_2 = (2.0)(4.0) = 8.0\ \mathrm{m/s}\).
  4. \(\boxed{v_2 = 8.0\ \mathrm{m/s}}\) — the area shrank by a factor of 4, so the speed grew by 4.
▶Example 4 — Buoyant force on a submerged object

A solid metal cube of side \(0.10\ \text{m}\) is fully submerged in water. Find the buoyant force on it. Use \(\rho_{water}=1000\ \mathrm{kg/m^3}\), \(g=9.8\ \mathrm{m/s^2}\).

  1. A fully submerged object displaces its entire volume: \(V = (0.10)^3 = 1.0\times10^{-3}\ \mathrm{m^3}\).
  2. Archimedes: \(F_b = \rho_{water}\, g\, V_{displaced}\).
  3. \(F_b = (1000)(9.8)(1.0\times10^{-3}) = 9.8\ \text{N}\).
  4. \(\boxed{F_b = 9.8\ \text{N}}\) — note this is independent of what metal the cube is made of.
▶Example 5 — Fraction of a floating block submerged

A block of wood with density \(\rho_{obj}=700\ \mathrm{kg/m^3}\) floats in water (\(\rho_{water}=1000\ \mathrm{kg/m^3}\)). What fraction of the block's volume is below the surface?

  1. Floating means buoyant force balances weight: \(\rho_{water}\, g\, V_{sub} = \rho_{obj}\, g\, V_{obj}\).
  2. Cancel \(g\) and solve for the submerged fraction: \(\dfrac{V_{sub}}{V_{obj}} = \dfrac{\rho_{obj}}{\rho_{water}}\).
  3. \(\dfrac{V_{sub}}{V_{obj}} = \dfrac{700}{1000} = 0.70\).
  4. \(\boxed{0.70}\) — 70% of the block sits below the waterline, which is why most of an iceberg is hidden underwater.
