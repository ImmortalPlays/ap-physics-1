1. What kinematics describes

Kinematics is the study of how things move — position, velocity and acceleration — without yet asking why (that's forces, in Unit 2). The three quantities you track are position \(x\), displacement \(\Delta x = x_f - x_i\) (a vector — direction matters), and distance (the total path length, always positive).

2. Velocity & speed

Average velocity is displacement over time; instantaneous velocity is the velocity at a single instant — the slope of the position–time graph.

$$v_{avg} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}$$

Speed is the magnitude of velocity (no direction). On an \(x\)–\(t\) graph, a steeper slope means a faster object; a negative slope means it's moving backward.

3. Acceleration

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity — the slope of the velocity–time graph:

$$a = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}$$

If \(a\) points the same way as \(v\), the object speeds up; if it points opposite, it slows down. A negative acceleration does not always mean "slowing down" — it depends on the direction of motion.

4. The kinematic equations

When acceleration is constant, these four equations connect displacement, velocity, acceleration and time. Pick the one that's missing the variable you don't have.

$$v = v_0 + at$$
$$\Delta x = v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}a t^2$$
$$v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\,\Delta x$$
$$\Delta x = \tfrac{1}{2}(v_0 + v)\,t$$

5. Motion graphs

  • Slope of \(x\)–\(t\) gives velocity.
  • Slope of \(v\)–\(t\) gives acceleration.
  • Area under \(v\)–\(t\) gives displacement. (The motion-graph simulation below shades this area live.)

6. Free fall

Near Earth's surface, gravity gives every object the same downward acceleration \(g \approx 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2\), regardless of mass. Going up, gravity slows the object; coming down, it speeds it up. The motion is symmetric: time up equals time down, and speed at a height matches on the way up and the way down.

7. Projectile motion

A projectile's horizontal and vertical motions are independent. Horizontally there's no acceleration (constant \(v_x\)); vertically it's just free fall.

  • Horizontal: \(x = v_0\cos\theta \cdot t\)
  • Vertical: \(y = v_0\sin\theta \cdot t - \tfrac{1}{2}g t^2\)

Launch angle sets the trade-off between range and height — explore it in the launcher below.

8. Vectors in two dimensions

A vector has magnitude and direction; a scalar (like distance or speed) has only magnitude. Any 2D vector splits into perpendicular components:

$$A_x = A\cos\theta, \qquad A_y = A\sin\theta$$

Add vectors by adding components separately, then recombine with \(A = \sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2}\) and \(\theta = \tan^{-1}(A_y/A_x)\). This component method is the backbone of every 2D problem in the course.

9. Reference frames & relative motion

Velocity is measured relative to a reference frame. To convert between frames, add velocities as vectors: the velocity of A relative to C is

$$\vec v_{A/C} = \vec v_{A/B} + \vec v_{B/C}$$

A swimmer crossing a river, or a person walking on a moving train, are classic examples — the ground-frame velocity is the vector sum of the two motions. Try it in the relative-motion sim below.

▶ Example 1 — Constant acceleration from rest

A car starts from rest and accelerates at \(a = 3\ \text{m/s}^2\) for \(t = 5\ \text{s}\). Find its final velocity and the distance traveled.

  1. List knowns: \(v_0 = 0,\ a = 3,\ t = 5\).
  2. Velocity: \(v = v_0 + at = 0 + 3(5) = \boxed{15\ \text{m/s}}\).
  3. Distance: \(\Delta x = v_0 t + \tfrac12 a t^2 = 0 + \tfrac12(3)(5)^2\).
  4. Simplify: \(\tfrac12(3)(25) = \boxed{37.5\ \text{m}}\).
▶ Example 2 — Free fall: a ball thrown straight up

A ball is thrown upward at \(v_0 = 20\ \text{m/s}\). How high does it go, and how long until it reaches the top? Use \(g = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2\).

  1. At the top, velocity is momentarily zero: \(v = 0\).
  2. Time up: \(0 = v_0 - g t \Rightarrow t = \dfrac{v_0}{g} = \dfrac{20}{9.8} \approx \boxed{2.04\ \text{s}}\).
  3. Max height: \(v^2 = v_0^2 - 2g\,\Delta y \Rightarrow 0 = 20^2 - 2(9.8)\Delta y\).
  4. Solve: \(\Delta y = \dfrac{400}{19.6} \approx \boxed{20.4\ \text{m}}\).
▶ Example 3 — Projectile range & height

A ball is launched at \(v_0 = 24\ \text{m/s}\) and \(\theta = 50^\circ\). Find the maximum height, time of flight, and range. (Set the launcher above to these values to watch it.)

  1. Components: \(v_x = 24\cos 50^\circ \approx 15.4\ \text{m/s}\), \(v_y = 24\sin 50^\circ \approx 18.4\ \text{m/s}\).
  2. Max height: \(\dfrac{v_y^2}{2g} = \dfrac{18.4^2}{19.6} \approx \boxed{17.3\ \text{m}}\).
  3. Flight time: \(T = \dfrac{2 v_y}{g} = \dfrac{2(18.4)}{9.8} \approx \boxed{3.76\ \text{s}}\).
  4. Range: \(v_x \cdot T = 15.4(3.76) \approx \boxed{57.9\ \text{m}}\).
▶ Example 4 — Displacement from a v–t graph

An object moves with velocity that rises linearly from \(0\) to \(12\ \text{m/s}\) over \(4\ \text{s}\), then stays constant at \(12\ \text{m/s}\) for \(3\ \text{s}\). Find total displacement.

  1. Displacement = area under the \(v\)–\(t\) graph.
  2. Triangle (0–4 s): \(\tfrac12 (4)(12) = 24\ \text{m}\).
  3. Rectangle (4–7 s): \(12 \times 3 = 36\ \text{m}\).
  4. Total: \(24 + 36 = \boxed{60\ \text{m}}\).
▶ Example 5 — Stopping distance

A car traveling at \(v_0 = 20\ \text{m/s}\) brakes with \(a = -5\ \text{m/s}^2\). How far does it travel before stopping?

  1. Final velocity is zero: \(v = 0\). Use \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\,\Delta x\).
  2. Substitute: \(0 = 20^2 + 2(-5)\Delta x\).
  3. Solve: \(\Delta x = \dfrac{-400}{-10}\).
  4. Answer: \(\boxed{40\ \text{m}}\).

1. Newton's three laws

  • 1st (inertia): an object keeps its velocity unless a net force acts on it.
  • 2nd: \(\vec F_{net} = m\vec a\) — net force sets acceleration.
  • 3rd: forces come in equal-and-opposite pairs on different objects.

2. Free-body diagrams & friction

Draw every force as an arrow leaving the object: weight \(mg\) down, normal \(N\) perpendicular to the surface, friction along it, plus any tension or applied force. Then add them as vectors.

Kinetic friction opposes sliding: \(f_k = \mu_k N\). Static friction adjusts itself up to a maximum \(f_{s,\max} = \mu_s N\) — nothing moves until you exceed it.

3. Inclined planes

Tilt your axes along the ramp. Gravity splits into a part along the surface, \(mg\sin\theta\) (what pulls it down the slope), and a part into it, \(mg\cos\theta\). The normal force balances the second: \(N = mg\cos\theta\).

4. Systems & center of mass

Treating several objects as one system lets you ignore internal forces and track just the center of mass — the average position of the mass:

$$x_{cm} = \frac{m_1 x_1 + m_2 x_2 + \dots}{m_1 + m_2 + \dots}$$

The center of mass accelerates as if the net external force acted on the total mass there — internal forces never move it on their own.

5. Gravitational & spring forces

Near Earth, weight is \(F_g = mg\). More generally, any two masses attract with Newton's law of gravitation: \(F_g = \dfrac{G m_1 m_2}{r^2}\).

A spring resists being stretched or compressed with Hooke's law: \(F_s = -kx\), where \(k\) is the spring constant and \(x\) the displacement from its natural length. The minus sign means the force always points back toward equilibrium.

6. Uniform circular motion

An object moving in a circle at constant speed is still accelerating, because its direction keeps changing. That centripetal acceleration points toward the center:

$$a_c = \frac{v^2}{r}, \qquad F_c = \frac{mv^2}{r}$$

The centripetal force isn't a new kind of force — it's whatever real force (tension, gravity, friction, the normal force) happens to point inward. Remove it and the object flies off along the tangent. Explore this in the circular-motion sim.

7. Connected systems & tension

When objects are linked by a rope over a pulley, they share the same magnitude of acceleration, and the rope tension is the same throughout (ideal massless rope). Two classic setups:

  • Atwood machine — two hanging masses; the heavier side falls with \(a = \dfrac{(m_2-m_1)g}{m_1+m_2}\).
  • Modified Atwood — a cart on a table pulled by one hanging mass. Only the hanging weight drives it, but the whole system has inertia: \(a = \dfrac{m_2 g - \mu m_1 g}{m_1+m_2}\). Try both in the sims.

Step-by-step solutions

▶Example 1 — Net force on a flat surface

A \(3\ \text{kg}\) box is pushed with \(20\ \text{N}\) against \(5\ \text{N}\) of friction. Find its acceleration.

  1. Net force: \(F_{net} = 20 - 5 = 15\ \text{N}\).
  2. Newton's 2nd law: \(a = \dfrac{F_{net}}{m} = \dfrac{15}{3}\).
  3. Answer: \(\boxed{5\ \text{m/s}^2}\).
▶Example 2 — Frictionless incline

A block slides down a frictionless ramp tilted at \(\theta = 30^\circ\). Find its acceleration.

  1. Only the along-ramp gravity acts: \(F = mg\sin\theta\).
  2. Divide by \(m\): \(a = g\sin\theta = 9.8\sin 30^\circ\).
  3. Answer: \(a = 9.8(0.5) = \boxed{4.9\ \text{m/s}^2}\).
▶Example 3 — Will it slide?

A block sits on a \(20^\circ\) incline with \(\mu_s = 0.5\). Does it start to slide?

  1. Driving force: \(mg\sin 20^\circ = 0.342\,mg\).
  2. Max static friction: \(\mu_s mg\cos 20^\circ = 0.5(0.940)mg = 0.470\,mg\).
  3. Since \(0.342 < 0.470\), friction wins — \(\boxed{\text{it stays put}}\).
▶Example 4 — Car on a flat curve

A \(1000\ \text{kg}\) car rounds a flat curve of radius \(50\ \text{m}\) at \(15\ \text{m/s}\). What centripetal force is needed, and what minimum \(\mu_s\) supplies it?

  1. Force needed: \(F_c = \dfrac{mv^2}{r} = \dfrac{1000(15)^2}{50} = \boxed{4500\ \text{N}}\).
  2. Friction must supply it: \(\mu_s mg \ge F_c\).
  3. So \(\mu_s \ge \dfrac{4500}{1000(9.8)} = \boxed{0.46}\).

1. Work & the work–energy theorem

Work is force times distance along the motion: \(W = Fd\cos\theta\). Push along the motion and \(W\) is positive; oppose it (like friction) and \(W\) is negative.

The net work done on an object equals its change in kinetic energy: \(W_{net} = \Delta KE\).

2. Kinetic & potential energy

$$KE = \tfrac12 m v^2$$
$$PE_g = mgh$$

A compressed or stretched spring stores \(PE_s = \tfrac12 k x^2\).

3. Conservation of energy & power

With no friction, mechanical energy is conserved: \(KE + PE = \text{constant}\). Energy just trades between the two forms — exactly what the skate-park sim shows. Power is how fast work is done: \(P = \dfrac{W}{t}\).

Step-by-step solutions

▶Example 1 — Speed at the bottom of a ramp

A cart starts from rest and drops \(h = 5\ \text{m}\) down a frictionless track. Find its speed at the bottom.

  1. Energy conservation: \(mgh = \tfrac12 m v^2\) (mass cancels).
  2. Solve: \(v = \sqrt{2gh} = \sqrt{2(9.8)(5)}\).
  3. Answer: \(v = \sqrt{98} \approx \boxed{9.9\ \text{m/s}}\).
▶Example 2 — Power to climb stairs

A \(60\ \text{kg}\) student climbs \(4\ \text{m}\) in \(8\ \text{s}\). Find the average power.

  1. Work against gravity: \(W = mgh = 60(9.8)(4) = 2352\ \text{J}\).
  2. Power: \(P = \dfrac{W}{t} = \dfrac{2352}{8}\).
  3. Answer: \(\boxed{294\ \text{W}}\).

1. Momentum & impulse

Momentum is mass in motion: \(\vec p = m\vec v\) (a vector). To change it you apply an impulse — a force over time:

$$\vec J = \vec F\,\Delta t = \Delta \vec p$$

2. Conservation of momentum

If no external force acts, the total momentum of a system is unchanged — before a collision equals after: \(\sum \vec p_i = \sum \vec p_f\). This holds even when kinetic energy is lost.

3. Elastic vs. inelastic

Elastic: objects bounce and kinetic energy is conserved. Inelastic: KE is lost to heat/sound; in a perfectly inelastic collision the objects stick together. Momentum is conserved in both cases.

Step-by-step solutions

▶Example 1 — Perfectly inelastic collision

A \(2\ \text{kg}\) cart at \(3\ \text{m/s}\) hits a stationary \(1\ \text{kg}\) cart and they stick. Find their common speed.

  1. Momentum before: \(2(3) + 1(0) = 6\ \mathrm{kg{\cdot}m/s}\).
  2. After, combined mass \(3\ \text{kg}\): \(6 = 3v\).
  3. Answer: \(v = \boxed{2\ \text{m/s}}\).
▶Example 2 — Impulse from a force

A \(0.5\ \text{kg}\) ball at \(4\ \text{m/s}\) is struck and leaves at \(4\ \text{m/s}\) the other way. The contact lasts \(0.02\ \text{s}\). Find the average force.

  1. Change in momentum: \(\Delta p = 0.5(-4) - 0.5(4) = -4\ \mathrm{kg{\cdot}m/s}\).
  2. Impulse–momentum: \(F = \dfrac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} = \dfrac{-4}{0.02}\).
  3. Answer: \(\boxed{-200\ \text{N}}\) (opposing the original motion).

1. Rotational kinematics

Rotation has its own kinematics, exactly mirroring the linear ones — just swap \(x\to\theta\), \(v\to\omega\) (angular velocity), \(a\to\alpha\) (angular acceleration):

$$\omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t$$
$$\theta = \omega_0 t + \tfrac12 \alpha t^2$$

2. Connecting linear & rotational motion

A point a distance \(r\) from the axis moves along an arc, so its linear and angular quantities are linked: \(v = r\omega\) and \(a_t = r\alpha\). The farther out you are, the faster you travel for the same spin rate — which is why the rim of a wheel outruns its hub.

3. Torque

Torque is the rotational "push" — force times lever arm: \(\tau = rF\sin\theta\). A force far from the pivot, applied perpendicular, twists hardest.

It's a vector: by convention counter-clockwise is positive.

4. Rotational inertia & Newton's 2nd law

Rotational inertia \(I\) measures how hard it is to change spin — mass farther from the axis counts more. The rotational version of \(F=ma\) is \(\tau_{net} = I\alpha\), where \(\alpha\) is angular acceleration.

5. Rotational equilibrium

An object balances when the torques cancel: \(\sum \tau = 0\) (and \(\sum F = 0\)). That's why a lighter child far from the pivot can balance a heavier one close in.

▶Example 1 — Balance the see-saw

A \(30\ \text{kg}\) child sits \(2\ \text{m}\) left of the pivot. How far right must a \(40\ \text{kg}\) child sit to balance?

  1. Balance means equal torques: \(m_1 g d_1 = m_2 g d_2\) (\(g\) cancels).
  2. Solve: \(d_2 = \dfrac{m_1 d_1}{m_2} = \dfrac{30(2)}{40}\).
  3. Answer: \(\boxed{1.5\ \text{m}}\).
▶Example 2 — Torque from an angled force

A \(50\ \text{N}\) force is applied \(0.8\ \text{m}\) from a hinge at \(30^\circ\) to the door. Find the torque.

  1. \(\tau = rF\sin\theta = 0.8(50)\sin 30^\circ\).
  2. \(= 40(0.5)\).
  3. Answer: \(\boxed{20\ \mathrm{N{\cdot}m}}\).

1. Angular momentum

The rotational analog of momentum: \(L = I\omega\). A fast, spread-out spin carries a lot of it.

2. Conservation of angular momentum

With no external torque, \(L\) stays constant. So if \(I\) drops, \(\omega\) must rise to compensate — the figure-skater effect: pull your arms in and you spin faster.

3. Rotational kinetic energy

A spinning object stores \(KE_{rot} = \tfrac12 I\omega^2\). Interestingly, when a skater pulls in, \(L\) is conserved but \(KE\) increases — the extra energy comes from the work their muscles do pulling the mass inward.

4. Rolling without slipping

When a wheel rolls without slipping, its translation and rotation are locked together: \(v = r\omega\). Its energy splits between moving and spinning:

$$KE_{total} = \tfrac12 m v^2 + \tfrac12 I\omega^2$$

Because some energy goes into spin, a rolling object accelerates down a ramp slower than a frictionless sliding one — and shapes with more inertia (hoops) lose the race to compact ones (disks). See the rolling-race sim.

5. Orbiting satellites

A satellite in circular orbit is in free fall — gravity supplies exactly the centripetal force it needs to curve around:

$$\frac{G M m}{r^2} = \frac{m v^2}{r} \;\Rightarrow\; v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}}$$

Higher orbits are slower and take longer — the orbital speed falls off as \(1/\sqrt{r}\). Notice the satellite's mass cancels entirely. Try it in the orbit sim.

▶Example 1 — Skater pulls in their arms

A skater spins at \(\omega_0 = 2\ \text{rad/s}\) with \(I_0 = 6\ \mathrm{kg{\cdot}m}^2\). They pull in to \(I_1 = 2\ \mathrm{kg{\cdot}m}^2\). Find the new spin rate.

  1. Conserve \(L\): \(I_0\omega_0 = I_1\omega_1\).
  2. \(\omega_1 = \dfrac{I_0\omega_0}{I_1} = \dfrac{6(2)}{2}\).
  3. Answer: \(\boxed{6\ \text{rad/s}}\) — three times faster.
▶Example 2 — Rotational kinetic energy

A disk with \(I = 0.5\ \mathrm{kg{\cdot}m}^2\) spins at \(\omega = 4\ \text{rad/s}\). Find its rotational KE.

  1. \(KE_{rot} = \tfrac12 I\omega^2 = \tfrac12(0.5)(4)^2\).
  2. \(= 0.25(16)\).
  3. Answer: \(\boxed{4\ \text{J}}\).

1. Simple harmonic motion

SHM happens whenever a restoring force pulls back toward equilibrium in proportion to the displacement: \(F = -kx\). The result is smooth, repeating back-and-forth motion.

2. Period formulas

$$T_{spring} = 2\pi\sqrt{\tfrac{m}{k}}$$
$$T_{pend} = 2\pi\sqrt{\tfrac{L}{g}}$$

Notice the pendulum's period depends only on length and gravity — not on the mass or (for small swings) the amplitude.

3. Energy in oscillation

Energy sloshes between kinetic and potential: fastest (max KE) at the center, momentarily still (max PE) at the turning points. Total stays constant if there's no friction.

1. Density & pressure

Density is how tightly mass is packed: \(\rho = \dfrac{m}{V}\). Pressure is force spread over area: \(P = \dfrac{F}{A}\).

In a fluid, pressure grows with depth: \(P = P_0 + \rho g h\).

2. Buoyancy (Archimedes' principle)

A submerged object feels an upward buoyant force equal to the weight of the fluid it pushes out of the way: \(F_b = \rho_{fluid}\, g\, V_{displaced}\).

3. Continuity & conservation of mass

An incompressible fluid can't pile up, so the volume flowing per second is the same everywhere in a pipe — the continuity equation:

$$A_1 v_1 = A_2 v_2$$

Squeeze the pipe to half the area and the fluid must move twice as fast. It's why a thumb over a hose nozzle makes the water shoot out. See the pipe-flow sim.

4. Fluids, Newton's laws & Bernoulli

Applying energy conservation to a flowing fluid gives Bernoulli's equation — pressure, speed and height trade off along a streamline:

$$P + \tfrac12 \rho v^2 + \rho g y = \text{constant}$$

The surprising consequence: where a fluid speeds up, its pressure drops. That low pressure over a faster-moving region is what lifts an airplane wing and curves a spinning ball.

5. Floating & sinking

An object floats if it's less dense than the fluid; the fraction submerged equals \(\dfrac{\rho_{obj}}{\rho_{fluid}}\). If it's denser, the buoyant force can't match its weight and it sinks.

Cheat Sheet

Every key equation, organized by the eight units of the 2024 CED.

1 · Kinematics

MOTION IN 1D & 2D
\(v = v_0 + at\)
\(\Delta x = v_0 t + \tfrac12 a t^2\)
\(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a\,\Delta x\)
Projectile: \(x=v_0\cos\theta\,t,\ y=v_0\sin\theta\,t-\tfrac12 g t^2\)
Vectors: \(A_x=A\cos\theta,\ A_y=A\sin\theta\)
Relative: \(\vec v_{A/C}=\vec v_{A/B}+\vec v_{B/C}\)

2 · Force & Dynamics

NEWTON'S LAWS, CIRCULAR, GRAVITY
\(\vec F_{net} = m\vec a\)
\(f_s \le \mu_s N,\quad f_k = \mu_k N\)
Incline: \(F_\parallel = mg\sin\theta,\ N = mg\cos\theta\)
Circular: \(a_c = \dfrac{v^2}{r},\ F_c = \dfrac{mv^2}{r}\)
Gravity: \(F_g = \dfrac{G m_1 m_2}{r^2}\)
Spring: \(F_s = -kx\)

3 · Work, Energy & Power

CONSERVATION OF ENERGY
\(W = Fd\cos\theta = \Delta KE\)
\(KE = \tfrac12 m v^2\)
\(PE_g = mgh,\quad PE_s = \tfrac12 k x^2\)
\(P = \dfrac{W}{t} = Fv\)

4 · Linear Momentum

COLLISIONS & IMPULSE
\(\vec p = m\vec v\)
Impulse: \(\vec J = \vec F\,\Delta t = \Delta \vec p\)
Conservation: \(\sum \vec p_i = \sum \vec p_f\)
Center of mass: \(x_{cm} = \dfrac{\sum m_i x_i}{\sum m_i}\)

5 · Torque & Rotation

ROTATIONAL DYNAMICS
\(\tau = rF\sin\theta = I\alpha\)
\(\omega = \omega_0 + \alpha t\)
\(\theta = \omega_0 t + \tfrac12 \alpha t^2\)
\(v = r\omega,\quad a_t = r\alpha\)
Equilibrium: \(\sum \tau = 0\)

6 · Rotating Systems

ANGULAR MOMENTUM & ORBITS
\(L = I\omega\)
Conservation: \(I_0\omega_0 = I_1\omega_1\)
\(KE_{rot} = \tfrac12 I\omega^2\)
Rolling: \(KE = \tfrac12 mv^2 + \tfrac12 I\omega^2\)
Orbit: \(v = \sqrt{\dfrac{GM}{r}}\)

7 · Oscillations

SIMPLE HARMONIC MOTION
\(F = -kx\)
\(T_{spring} = 2\pi\sqrt{\dfrac{m}{k}}\)
\(T_{pendulum} = 2\pi\sqrt{\dfrac{L}{g}}\)
\(f = \dfrac{1}{T}\)

8 · Fluids

PRESSURE, BUOYANCY & FLOW
\(\rho = \dfrac{m}{V},\quad P = \dfrac{F}{A}\)
\(P = P_0 + \rho g h\)
Buoyancy: \(F_b = \rho g V\)
Continuity: \(A_1 v_1 = A_2 v_2\)
Bernoulli: \(P + \tfrac12 \rho v^2 + \rho g y = \text{const}\)

Constants

PROVIDED ON THE EXAM
\(g = 9.8\ \text{m/s}^2\)
\(G = 6.67\times10^{-11}\ \mathrm{N{\cdot}m^2/kg^2}\)
\(\rho_{water} = 1000\ \text{kg/m}^3\)
\(P_{atm} = 1.0\times10^{5}\ \text{Pa}\)

FAQ

Common questions about the AP Physics 1 exam.

▶What score do I need?

The exam is scored 1–5. A 3 is "qualified" and generally passing; many colleges grant credit for a 4 or 5. Always check the policy of the college you're aiming for.

▶How is the exam structured?

Two sections, each worth 50%: 40 multiple-choice questions (80 min) and 4 free-response questions (100 min). A calculator and a formula sheet are provided for both sections.

▶Is it algebra-based or calculus-based?

Algebra-based. You won't need calculus — just algebra, trigonometry, and clear reasoning. The calculus-based courses are AP Physics C (Mechanics and E&M).

▶Do I need to memorize formulas?

A formula sheet is provided, so raw memorization matters less than in some courses. What matters is knowing which equation applies and why — exactly what the simulations are built to teach.

▶What's the hardest part for most students?

The free-response and the experimental-design / reasoning questions — they ask you to explain physics in words and design experiments, not just compute. Practice writing clear, step-by-step justifications.
